# Verdant Compensation and Vesting Framework

## Purpose

This framework explains how people and businesses may be compensated within the Verdant Enterprise Network and how future vesting, ownership, profit-sharing, or strategic participation may be considered later.

The purpose is to create opportunity without making premature promises.

This document is a planning framework only. It is not an employment agreement, contractor agreement, equity plan, profit-sharing plan, securities offering, trust document, partnership agreement, or legal commitment.

Any future equity, vesting, ownership, bonus, profit-sharing, or exit participation must be reviewed by an attorney and CPA and documented through formal agreements before it becomes real.

## Master Principle

> Compensation is earned through real work, real responsibility, real risk, real contribution, or lawful ownership.

Do not promise ownership casually.

Do not use nonprofit funds for private enrichment.

Do not disguise personal support as business compensation.

## Compensation Categories

The network may include several types of compensation depending on the role, entity, and legal structure.

### 1. Wages

Wages are paid to employees through payroll.

Use wages when a person:

- Works under direction and control
- Has assigned hours
- Uses employer systems or equipment
- Performs ongoing operational work
- Should legally be treated as an employee

Requirements:

- Job description
- Pay rate
- Time records
- Payroll setup
- Tax withholding
- Worker classification review
- Proper employer entity

### 2. Contractor Payments

Contractor payments are paid to independent individuals or businesses for defined services.

Use contractor payments when the provider:

- Controls how work is performed
- Provides a defined service or deliverable
- Has business independence
- Invoices for work
- May serve other clients
- Maintains separate records

Requirements:

- W-9 or appropriate tax form
- Written scope of work
- Invoice
- Payment record
- Deliverable or activity record
- Classification review

### 3. Vendor Payments

Vendor payments are paid for goods, supplies, equipment, professional services, rentals, technology, or other business needs.

Requirements:

- Invoice or receipt
- Business purpose
- Correct paying entity
- Approval where required
- Contract if recurring or material

### 4. Management Fees

Management fees are paid by clients to Verdant Management Partners under written service agreements.

Requirements:

- Client service agreement
- Scope of services
- Fee schedule
- Approval by client authority
- Invoice
- Work records
- Conflict review where applicable

### 5. Related-Party Payments

Related-party payments involve family members, insider-owned businesses, board members, officers, or entities controlled by people connected to the decision-makers.

Examples:

- VMP pays Verdant Ventures LLC.
- CGN pays VMP.
- A nonprofit pays a family-owned vendor.
- A client pays a company owned by a partner or board member.

Related-party payments must be documented more carefully than ordinary vendor payments.

Requirements:

- Conflict disclosure
- Clear business need
- Written agreement
- Reasonable pricing
- Independent approval where required
- Invoice
- Deliverable record
- Payment record
- Periodic review

### 6. Reimbursements

Reimbursements repay someone for a legitimate expense they paid on behalf of an entity.

Requirements:

- Receipt
- Date
- Amount
- Business purpose
- Correct entity
- Approval

Reimbursements are not emergency cash advances or hidden compensation.

### 7. Owner Draws and Distributions

Owner draws or distributions may be available from for-profit entities depending on ownership, tax treatment, cash flow, and operating agreements.

Requirements:

- Ownership interest
- Proper entity records
- CPA review
- Tax planning
- Compliance with operating agreement
- No misuse of restricted or nonprofit funds

Nonprofit organizations do not distribute profits to private owners.

## Compensation Standards

Compensation should be:

- Reasonable
- Documented
- Connected to real work or ownership
- Approved by the correct authority
- Paid by the correct entity
- Reported correctly for tax and benefits purposes
- Reviewed periodically

## Compensation Should Not Be

Compensation should not be:

- A gift disguised as wages
- A family support payment disguised as contractor pay
- A nonprofit benefit to insiders
- A fake invoice
- A payment for work not performed
- A way to move restricted funds improperly
- A way to hide income from benefits agencies
- A promise of future ownership without legal documentation

## Provider Development Philosophy

The network should help service providers build legitimate businesses.

A provider may leave the network after a year with:

- Business bank records
- Invoices
- Tax records
- Bookkeeping history
- Client references
- Service descriptions
- Proof of performance
- Operating procedures
- Better credit readiness
- Stronger confidence

That outcome is still a success even if the provider never receives equity, vesting, or exit participation.

## Opportunity Levels

The network may recognize different stages of contribution.

### Level 1: Helper / Volunteer

Contributes unpaid support to mission work where legally appropriate.

No ownership expectation.

### Level 2: Task-Based Paid Worker

Completes specific tasks for pay.

May be employee or contractor depending on facts.

No ownership expectation.

### Level 3: Employee

Works for an entity under payroll.

May be eligible for raises, benefits, bonuses, or advancement depending on the employer.

No automatic ownership expectation.

### Level 4: Independent Contractor

Provides defined services under contract.

Builds work history and business records.

No automatic ownership expectation.

### Level 5: Service Provider Business

Operates as a real business with its own records, bank account, service menu, and invoices.

May become a recurring vendor or preferred provider.

No automatic ownership expectation.

### Level 6: Preferred Provider

Demonstrates reliability, quality, compliance, and professionalism over time.

May receive more consistent opportunities, referrals, or longer-term contracts.

No automatic ownership expectation.

### Level 7: Strategic Partner

Contributes materially to growth, systems, client development, program design, revenue development, or network capacity.

May be considered for special agreements, bonuses, revenue-sharing, project participation, or future ownership discussions where legally appropriate.

No ownership exists unless documented.

### Level 8: Future Equity or Vesting Participant

May participate in a legally reviewed equity, phantom equity, profit interest, bonus pool, revenue-share, or exit-participation plan.

This level requires formal legal and tax structure.

## Future Vesting Philosophy

Vesting should reward sustained contribution, not early enthusiasm.

Future vesting may be considered for people or businesses that provide long-term value, carry responsibility, build systems, generate revenue, protect compliance, or help create enterprise value.

Possible factors:

- Length of contribution
- Quality of contribution
- Revenue generated
- Systems built
- Client relationships developed
- Leadership demonstrated
- Risk carried
- Capital contributed
- Compliance record
- Reliability under pressure
- Ability to build others

## What May Be Vested Later

Future plans may involve one or more of the following, depending on legal structure:

- Equity in a for-profit entity
- Membership interests in an LLC
- Profit interests
- Phantom equity
- Revenue-share agreements
- Performance bonuses
- Exit bonuses
- Referral fees
- Strategic partner pools
- Project-based participation
- Intellectual property royalty arrangements

Nonprofit assets, nonprofit control, charitable funds, and restricted gaming proceeds cannot be casually vested to private individuals.

## Verdant Holdings LLC

Verdant Holdings LLC may be formed later for:

- Holding equity interests
- Holding intellectual property
- Holding strategic assets
- Preparing for acquisition activity
- Preparing for future sale or exit planning
- Separating operating companies from long-term assets

No person should be promised ownership in Verdant Holdings before the entity exists, has a legal purpose, and has formal agreements.

## Exit Participation

Exit participation means a person or business may receive some benefit if a company, asset, brand, or project is sold, merged, licensed, acquired, or otherwise monetized.

Exit participation must be defined in writing before it is relied on.

Questions to answer before creating any exit plan:

- Which entity owns the asset?
- Which asset is being sold?
- Who contributed to building the asset?
- Was the contribution paid already?
- Is additional reward appropriate?
- Is the participant an employee, contractor, owner, or vendor?
- Are nonprofit assets involved?
- Are charitable gaming funds involved?
- Are securities laws involved?
- What tax consequences apply?

## Bonus Framework

Before formal equity exists, the network may consider simpler reward structures.

Possible bonuses:

- Performance bonus
- Project completion bonus
- Client acquisition bonus
- Retention bonus
- Compliance excellence bonus
- Profitability bonus
- Crisis recovery bonus
- Systems-building bonus

Bonus rules should be written before bonuses are paid.

Bonus payments must be tied to the paying entity's legal authority and financial capacity.

## Revenue Sharing

Revenue sharing may be useful for specific projects but must be handled carefully.

Revenue-share agreements should define:

- Revenue source
- Gross or net calculation
- Expenses deducted
- Payment timing
- Term
- Cap, if any
- Termination rules
- Tax treatment
- Ownership of client relationship
- Ownership of intellectual property

Do not use revenue sharing to avoid payroll, taxes, nonprofit restrictions, or charitable gaming restrictions.

## Intellectual Property

Before compensation or vesting is tied to a brand, website, campaign, process, or content, identify who owns the intellectual property.

Questions:

- Who created it?
- Who paid for it?
- Was it created for a nonprofit?
- Was it created under contract?
- Was it created as employee work?
- Was it created by a contractor?
- Was there a written IP assignment?
- Can it be licensed instead of transferred?

IP ownership must be documented before any exit or vesting discussion becomes serious.

## Family Member Compensation

Family members may be paid for real work, but family status cannot replace documentation.

Family compensation should follow the same standards as any other compensation:

- Real role
- Real work
- Reasonable rate
- Proper entity
- Time or deliverable records
- Tax reporting
- Benefits reporting
- Conflict review where needed

## Nonprofit Compensation Caution

Nonprofit compensation must be reasonable and mission-related.

Nonprofit funds cannot be used for:

- Private enrichment
- Personal lifestyle support
- Hidden profit distribution
- Excessive compensation
- Unrelated family support
- Insider payments without review

If a nonprofit pays VMP, Verdant Ventures, a family member, or any related provider, the payment should be reviewed carefully and approved through the proper nonprofit process.

## Charitable Gaming Caution

Charitable gaming money requires special care.

Before using any gaming-related funds for compensation, management fees, rent, vendor payments, staffing, or administration, confirm:

- Who is legally allowed to conduct gaming activity
- What expenses are allowed
- Who may be paid
- What records are required
- Whether related-party payments are allowed
- Whether board approval is required
- Whether regulatory reporting is required

## No Promise Clause

Participation in the Verdant Enterprise Network does not guarantee:

- Employment
- Continued work
- Preferred provider status
- Bonuses
- Revenue sharing
- Ownership
- Vesting
- Equity
- Exit payout
- Board seat
- Management authority

Any such rights must be created by a separate written agreement.

## Annual Review

Compensation and future participation should be reviewed at least annually.

Review:

- Pay rates
- Contractor classification
- Related-party payments
- Provider performance
- Client profitability
- Nonprofit compliance
- Gaming compliance
- Tax reporting
- Benefits impacts
- Bonus eligibility
- Whether any future vesting plan is appropriate yet

## Plain-English Summary

Pay people for real work.

Pay businesses for real services.

Document everything.

Do not promise ownership until lawyers and accountants structure it properly.

Do not use nonprofit or gaming money casually.

A year of clean records and real work can be valuable even without equity.

The ladder is opportunity, not entitlement.
