# Verdant Partner Handbook

## Purpose

This handbook explains how organizations, service providers, vendors, contractors, and strategic partners can participate in the Verdant Enterprise Network.

The goal is to create a shared standard for professionalism, documentation, accountability, and mutual benefit.

This handbook is a draft planning tool. It does not replace contracts, employment agreements, vendor agreements, bylaws, operating agreements, or legal advice.

## What the Network Is

The Verdant Enterprise Network is a collaborative ecosystem of legally separate organizations that work together through written agreements and shared standards.

The network may include:

- Nonprofit organizations
- For-profit businesses
- Independent service providers
- Contractors
- Vendors
- Employees
- Volunteers
- Strategic partners
- Future incubated businesses

## What the Network Is Not

The network is not:

- One combined company
- One combined nonprofit
- A way to mix personal and business funds
- A way to bypass nonprofit restrictions
- A way to disguise employment as contracting
- A promise of equity, ownership, or future payout
- A substitute for proper contracts and legal structure

## Participation Categories

### Client Organization

A client organization pays Verdant Management Partners for services under a written agreement.

Examples:

- Common Good Network
- Potent C710
- Wonderland Wellness World
- Kentucky NORML
- Tennessee NORML
- Future nonprofit or for-profit clients

### Service Provider

A service provider performs work for VMP, a client, or another network participant.

Examples:

- Verdant Ventures LLC
- Marketing providers
- Food and beverage providers
- Janitorial providers
- Maintenance providers
- Technology providers
- Bookkeeping providers
- Event support providers

### Strategic Partner

A strategic partner has a deeper relationship with the network and may help develop programs, clients, systems, or future ventures.

Strategic partner status must be earned and documented. It is not automatic.

### Volunteer

A volunteer may support mission-based nonprofit work but must not be used to avoid wage laws or substitute for paid labor where payment is required.

### Employee

An employee works under the direction and control of an employer and should be paid through the correct payroll process.

## Entry Standards

Before joining the network, a partner should be able to identify:

- Legal name
- Contact person
- Business or organization type
- Ownership or governance structure
- Services offered or services needed
- Relevant licenses or registrations
- Insurance needs
- Tax form needs
- Conflict-of-interest issues
- Bank account status
- Basic bookkeeping process

Not every early-stage provider will have everything perfect on day one. The point is to know what exists, what is missing, and what must be fixed before money or responsibility increases.

## Required Records for Service Providers

Service providers should maintain:

- Separate bank account where practical
- Tax identification information
- W-9 or appropriate tax form
- Invoices
- Receipts
- Basic bookkeeping
- Written scope of work
- Work records or deliverables
- Insurance if needed
- Licenses or permits if needed

## Required Records for Client Organizations

Client organizations should maintain:

- Formation documents
- EIN and tax records
- Bank accounts
- Governance records
- Contracts
- Invoices
- Financial records
- Program records
- Compliance records
- Licenses and permits
- Board approvals where applicable

## How a Provider Joins

Recommended process:

1. Identify the service offered.
2. Confirm whether the provider is an individual, LLC, corporation, nonprofit, or informal startup.
3. Collect tax and contact information.
4. Identify conflicts of interest.
5. Confirm whether the role is employee, contractor, vendor, or volunteer.
6. Draft a scope of work.
7. Set payment terms.
8. Confirm recordkeeping expectations.
9. Sign a written agreement.
10. Begin with a limited trial or defined project.

## How a Client Joins

Recommended process:

1. Identify client needs.
2. Confirm the client's legal status and authority to contract.
3. Identify decision-makers.
4. Clarify the scope of VMP services.
5. Identify compliance concerns.
6. Draft a service agreement.
7. Confirm fees and payment terms.
8. Obtain board or owner approval where required.
9. Begin work only after agreement and approval are complete.

## Expectations for All Participants

Participants are expected to:

- Act professionally.
- Communicate clearly.
- Keep commitments.
- Maintain records.
- Invoice honestly.
- Pay honestly.
- Disclose conflicts.
- Respect confidentiality.
- Respect legal boundaries.
- Avoid gossip, exploitation, and informal side deals.
- Solve problems directly when possible.

## Network Values

The network values:

- Competence
- Reliability
- Transparency
- Community benefit
- Family stability
- Disability-aware and trauma-aware work practices
- Entrepreneurial independence
- Practical compliance
- Long-term thinking

## Opportunity Pathway

The network should help people move upward when they are ready.

Possible pathway:

1. Helper / volunteer
2. Paid task worker
3. Part-time worker
4. Contractor
5. Service provider
6. Preferred provider
7. Strategic partner
8. Future venture participant
9. Mentor for others

Movement through the pathway is based on performance, records, reliability, fit, and legal structure. It is not automatic.

## Provider Development Goals

When possible, VMP should help providers build:

- Service menus
- Pricing sheets
- Invoicing habits
- Bookkeeping habits
- Business bank records
- Business credit readiness
- Client references
- Operating procedures
- Insurance readiness
- Tax readiness
- Professional identity

This allows providers to leave stronger even if they do not remain in the network long-term.

## Performance Standards

Providers should be evaluated based on:

- Quality of work
- Reliability
- Communication
- Documentation
- Timeliness
- Compliance awareness
- Client satisfaction
- Ability to follow scope
- Ability to solve problems
- Financial and operational maturity

## Grounds for Pause or Removal

A participant may be paused or removed for:

- Misuse of funds
- False invoices
- Poor performance
- Repeated missed deadlines
- Unsafe conduct
- Confidentiality breaches
- Refusal to document work
- Hidden conflicts of interest
- Misrepresentation of credentials
- Illegal activity
- Conduct that threatens nonprofit or gaming compliance

## Confidentiality

Participants may access sensitive information, including:

- Client records
- Financial information
- Business plans
- Nonprofit records
- Personal family information
- Health or benefits-related information
- Vendor records
- Donor or customer information

Confidential information must not be shared casually or used for personal advantage.

## Dispute Resolution

Recommended process:

1. Direct conversation if safe and appropriate.
2. Written summary of the issue.
3. Review the contract or scope.
4. Identify desired resolution.
5. Escalate to VMP or proper entity authority.
6. Pause work if compliance, safety, or money is at risk.
7. Document the outcome.

## Plain-English Summary

The network is built to help people and organizations grow.

But growth requires clean records, real work, clear contracts, and honest communication.

Participation creates opportunity, not entitlement.

The goal is for every serious participant to become more capable, more credible, and more independent over time.
