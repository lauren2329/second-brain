# Verdant Governance Framework

## Purpose

This framework explains how decisions should be made across the Verdant Enterprise Network while preserving legal separation between nonprofit organizations, for-profit businesses, service providers, and personal household finances.

The purpose is to create enough structure for trust, accountability, and compliance without pretending that every network participant is part of one legal entity.

This is a planning document. It must be reviewed by legal counsel, a CPA, and each organization's proper governing authority before being treated as final policy.

## Governance Philosophy

Verdant governance is based on clear roles, clean records, written agreements, and documented decision-making.

The network should avoid informal arrangements when money, authority, nonprofit assets, charitable gaming funds, client records, family members, related parties, or public trust are involved.

## Core Rules

1. Each legal entity governs itself.
2. Each entity keeps separate records, funds, contracts, and authority.
3. Written agreements are required before services begin whenever money will change hands.
4. Related-party transactions must be disclosed and documented.
5. Nonprofit decisions must follow nonprofit governance rules.
6. Charitable gaming decisions must follow charitable gaming law and license requirements.
7. No person should approve their own compensation without proper independent review.
8. No entity should be used to conceal income, assets, expenses, or control.
9. If a decision feels hard to explain to a regulator, lender, attorney, CPA, partner, or board member, pause and document it before acting.

## Decision-Making Levels

### Level 1: Routine Operational Decisions

Routine decisions may be handled by the responsible manager, employee, contractor, or vendor.

Examples:

- Scheduling
- Basic purchasing within budget
- Routine client communications
- Inventory orders
- Task assignment
- Ordinary project coordination
- Routine social media or website updates within approved guidelines

Recommended documentation:

- Email or task record
- Receipt if money is spent
- Invoice if vendor work is involved
- Notes in the appropriate project file

### Level 2: Management Decisions

Management decisions should be approved by the person or entity responsible for the contract, client relationship, or program.

Examples:

- New service scopes
- Staffing changes
- Contractor assignments
- Vendor selection
- Budget changes
- Program changes
- Client reporting commitments
- Marketing strategy
- New recurring expenses

Recommended documentation:

- Written approval
- Updated budget or scope
- Contract addendum if needed
- Meeting note or decision memo

### Level 3: Governance Decisions

Governance decisions require formal approval by the proper entity authority.

Examples:

- Nonprofit board approval
- Related-party contracts
- Officer compensation
- Management agreements involving nonprofit funds
- Charitable gaming arrangements
- Major leases
- Debt
- Equity grants
- Asset transfers
- Formation of new entities
- Intellectual property transfers
- Mergers, acquisitions, or exits

Recommended documentation:

- Board minutes or member consent
- Conflict disclosure
- Legal review where needed
- CPA review where needed
- Signed agreement
- Stored copy in the correct entity file

## Conflict-of-Interest Standard

A conflict of interest exists when a person involved in a decision may personally benefit from that decision or has a close relationship with someone who benefits.

Conflicts are not automatically prohibited. Hidden conflicts are the problem.

Examples:

- CGN pays VMP while VMP is controlled by people close to CGN leadership.
- VMP pays Verdant Ventures, owned by Lauren and Matthew.
- A nonprofit pays a family-owned vendor.
- A board member's business receives a contract.
- A person helps set their own pay rate.

## Conflict Review Process

When a conflict may exist:

1. Disclose the relationship.
2. Identify who benefits.
3. Confirm the service or transaction is needed.
4. Document the proposed price or compensation.
5. Compare to market pricing when possible.
6. Have disinterested decision-makers approve where required.
7. Record the decision.
8. Keep invoices, contracts, and deliverables.

## Related-Party Contract Requirements

Any related-party contract should include:

- Parties
- Scope of work
- Payment terms
- Deliverables
- Term
- Termination rights
- Confidentiality terms
- Data ownership terms
- Conflict disclosure
- Approval record
- Invoicing requirements

## Board Governance for Nonprofits

Nonprofit organizations in the network must remain board-governed.

VMP may provide administration or management support, but VMP should not replace the nonprofit board's legal authority.

Nonprofit boards should approve:

- Management agreements
- Related-party transactions
- Officer compensation
- Major contracts
- Charitable gaming structures
- Major leases
- Restricted fund use
- Mission-critical programming
- Annual budgets
- Financial reports

## Charitable Gaming Governance

Any charitable gaming activity must be governed according to the applicable license, state law, local rules, and nonprofit requirements.

Special care is required for:

- Who conducts gaming activities
- Who handles proceeds
- What expenses may be paid
- What management support is allowed
- How records are kept
- How proceeds are used
- Whether related-party payments are permitted
- What reports must be filed

Golden Door cannot be treated like an ordinary commercial lounge if it is operating as a charitable gaming program.

## Recordkeeping Standards

Each entity should maintain:

- Formation documents
- EIN and tax records
- Bank records
- Contracts
- Invoices
- Receipts
- Meeting notes or minutes
- Payroll records
- Contractor files
- Insurance records
- Licenses and permits
- Board approvals where applicable
- Conflict disclosures
- Client deliverables
- Financial reports

## Governance Calendar

Recommended recurring rhythm:

### Monthly

- Review bank statements.
- Review invoices and payments.
- Review related-party payments.
- Review contracts in progress.
- Review compliance deadlines.

### Quarterly

- Review budgets.
- Review service agreements.
- Review provider performance.
- Review conflicts of interest.
- Review tax and benefits issues.

### Annually

- Update entity map.
- Review bylaws and operating agreements.
- Review insurance.
- Review compensation.
- Review tax filings.
- Review contractor classifications.
- Review network strategy.

## Escalation Rule

Pause and escalate for attorney or CPA review when a decision involves:

- Nonprofit money paid to insiders
- Charitable gaming proceeds
- Related-party compensation
- New entity formation
- Debt
- Equity or vesting
- Trust planning
- Benefits eligibility
- Asset transfers
- Intellectual property transfers
- Major leases
- Any structure that is difficult to explain plainly

## Plain-English Summary

Each entity governs itself.

VMP coordinates services but does not erase the legal boundaries between organizations.

Conflicts are disclosed, not hidden.

Major decisions are documented.

Nonprofit and gaming matters get extra review.

The network can move quickly only if the records stay clean.
