## Overview

Advocacy is NORML's core mission. This toolkit gives every chapter a process for building legislative relationships, responding to bills, writing testimony, and engaging coalitions — before there's a crisis, not after.

---

## 501(c)(4) Advocacy vs. Lobbying — Know the Line

This is the most important compliance issue for NORML chapters.

| Activity | 501(c)(4) Status |
|---|---|
| Educating the public about cannabis policy | ✅ Permitted — unlimited |
| Urging supporters to contact legislators | ✅ Permitted |
| Testifying at legislative hearings | ✅ Permitted |
| Direct lobbying (telling legislators how to vote) | ✅ Permitted — but must not be the PRIMARY activity |
| Endorsing or opposing specific candidates | ❌ Not permitted |
| Making contributions to candidates or PACs | ❌ Not permitted |
| Coordinating with a candidate's campaign | ❌ Not permitted |

**The rule:** A 501(c)(4) can lobby — it's just not the majority of what the org does. Education, advocacy, and community work must be the primary activity. Keep records of time and resources spent to document the ratio if ever questioned.

**When in doubt, consult a nonprofit attorney.** VMP can connect chapters with appropriate legal resources.

## Building Legislative Relationships

The biggest mistake chapters make: only contacting legislators when a bill is moving. By then it's too late.

### Build the Relationship Before You Need It

1. **Map your delegation** — identify every state senator and representative in your district and your state. Note their party, cannabis position (supportive / opposed / unknown), and committee assignments (especially judiciary, health, and agriculture committees)

2. **Request introductory meetings** — reach out to the scheduler for a 15-minute introductory meeting. Frame it as: "We'd like to introduce [Chapter Name] and share what we're hearing from cannabis patients in your district." No ask on the first meeting.

3. **Be a resource, not a pest** — send relevant research, patient stories, and BillTrack50 updates to offices that are persuadable. You want to be the first call they make when they have a cannabis question.

4. **Show up** — attend town halls, committee hearings (even on unrelated bills), and community events where legislators appear. Being visible matters.

5. **Invite them to your conference** — KYCannaCon and equivalent state conferences are perfect low-pressure opportunities for legislators to engage with the cannabis community on neutral ground.

### Legislator Tracker

Maintain in Google Sheets, updated each session:

| Name | Chamber | District | Party | Cannabis Position | Key Committee | Last Contact | Next Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | | | | |

**Cannabis position codes:**
- S = Supportive (has voted for or sponsored cannabis bills)
- O = Opposed (has voted against or spoken against)
- U = Unknown (no record)
- P = Persuadable (mixed record or expressed openness)

## Action Alerts

National NORML distributes action alerts when federal legislation moves. State chapters distribute localized versions.

### State-Level Action Alert Process

1. Bill is introduced or moves in committee → BillTrack50 alert fires
2. Chapter director reviews the bill and determines position
3. Draft localized action alert using Action Network:
   - Subject: `[URGENT] [Bill name/number] needs your voice — contact [legislators] now`
   - Body: What the bill does, why it matters to [state] patients, exactly who to contact and how
   - One-click email to legislators embedded in Action Network
4. Send to full email list
5. Post to social
6. Report participation numbers to national NORML

### Federal Action Alert Localization

When national NORML sends an action alert on a federal bill:
- Identify the specific Kentucky/state federal delegation contacts
- Customize the message with state-specific patient context
- Send within 24 hours of national alert

## Testimony Writing Guide

When a cannabis bill has a committee hearing, chapters should submit written testimony and, when possible, send a speaker.

### Written Testimony Format

**Header:**
> Submitted by: [Chapter Name]
> Contact: [Name, email, phone]
> RE: [Bill number and name]
> Position: [Support / Oppose / Amend]

**Paragraph 1 — Who you are:**
One sentence. Organization name, mission, how long you've operated, who you represent.

**Paragraph 2 — Your position:**
State it clearly. "We support / oppose [bill] because..."

**Paragraphs 3–4 — Evidence:**
Patient stories, research citations, data on cannabis use in the state, public health evidence. Two sources minimum. Be specific — "X% of [state] residents support medical cannabis" is stronger than "many people support this."

**Paragraph 5 — The ask:**
Exactly what you want the committee to do. Vote yes. Vote no. Amend the bill to include/exclude [specific provision].

**Close:**
Thank the committee. Offer to answer questions. Contact information.

**Length:** 1–2 pages maximum. Committees receive dozens of submissions. Be clear and brief.

### Oral Testimony Tips

- You usually get 2–3 minutes. Prepare for 2.
- Open by stating your name, organization, and position on the bill
- Tell one patient story — real, specific, humanizing
- State your ask clearly
- Thank the committee
- Practice out loud before you go

## Coalition Building

### Who to Build Relationships With

| Partner Type | Why They Matter |
|---|---|
| Cannabis dispensaries and retailers | They have customers who are patients — shared audience, potential sponsors |
| Hemp and CBD businesses | Adjacent industry, similar regulatory battles |
| Patient advocacy organizations | Chronic illness, pain, PTSD, cancer — overlapping constituencies |
| Criminal justice reform organizations | Shared interest in decriminalization and expungement |
| Civil liberties organizations (ACLU) | Constitutional and rights framing |
| Medical and nursing associations | Credibility when they support cannabis access |
| Faith communities | Surprising allies on compassionate care framing |
| Labor unions | Workers affected by drug testing policies |
| Other state NORML chapters | Intelligence sharing, coordinated campaigns |

### Coalition Principles

- Build before you need — don't call for help when you've never offered it
- Show up for their issues — coalition is reciprocal
- Don't compromise your position to keep a coalition partner — agree on what you agree on, acknowledge what you don't
- Formalize relationships with an MOU (Memorandum of Understanding) for major ongoing partnerships

## Smoke the Vote — State Playbook

National NORML's Smoke the Vote campaign mobilizes cannabis supporters to register and vote.

### State Chapter Responsibilities

1. Register with national NORML at campaign launch
2. Identify [state]-specific voter registration deadlines and key races
3. Identify candidates with clear cannabis policy positions — use public record (voting history, public statements), never endorse
4. Distribute voter registration links via email (Action Network) and social
5. Host or promote voter registration events
6. Track and report registration numbers to national NORML
7. On Election Day — remind your list to vote via email and social

**Reminder:** Do not endorse candidates. Distribute factual information about candidates' cannabis policy records and let supporters decide.
